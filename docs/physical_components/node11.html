Node 11: Vibration and Shock Detector

This node is powered by a Raspberry Pi Pico W, uses vibration and shock sensors to monitor industrial machinery and infrastructure for potential damages. It plays a critical role in preventative maintenance and safety by providing early warnings, thus preventing equipment failure and extending asset lifetimes through timely interventions.

Components Overview​

Controller: Raspberry Pi Pico W​

The Raspberry Pi Pico W is a robust microcontroller designed for projects requiring low-power consumption and cost-effectiveness. It integrates a dual-core ARM Cortex-M0+ CPU with a peak frequency of 133 MHz, and is equipped with 264KB of RAM and 2MB of Flash memory. With on-board wireless capabilities including 2.4GHz Wi-Fi and Bluetooth, it supports a wide range of applications from IoT to automation and sensor networks. The array of GPIO pins and analog inputs makes it versatile for interfacing with various sensors and actuators.

Technical Specifications and Features​

  • Microcontroller: RP2040, with 2MB of flash memory.
  • Wireless Interfaces: Single-band 2.4GHz wireless interfaces supporting 802.11n, Bluetooth 5.2, LE Central and Peripheral roles, and Classic Bluetooth.
  • Connectivity:
    • Micro USB B port for power, data, and programming.
    • 40-pin DIP-style PCB with edge castellations.
    • 26 multi-function 3.3V GPIO pins, 23 digital-only and three ADC capable.
  • Programming and Debugging:
    • 3-pin Arm serial wire debug (SWD) port.
  • Power Supply:
    • Flexible power supply options via micro USB, external sources, or batteries.
  • Cost and Availability: High quality and availability at a low cost.
  • Software and Documentation: Comprehensive SDK, with various software examples and detailed documentation.

Key Features​

  • Processor: Dual-core Cortex M0+ at up to 133MHz, with an on-chip PLL for variable core frequency.
  • Memory:
    • 264kB multi-bank high-performance SRAM.
    • External Quad-SPI flash with XIP and 16kB on-chip cache.
  • USB Support: On-board USB1.1 (device or host).
  • GPIO:
    • 30 multi-function general purpose I/O (4 usable for ADC).
  • ADC: 12-bit 500ksps ADC.
  • Digital Peripherals:
    • 2 × UART, 2 × I2C, 2 × SPI, 16 × PWM channels.
    • Timer with 4 alarms, real-time clock.
  • Programmable I/O: 2 × PIO blocks, 8 state machines in total, capable of emulating SD card and VGA interfaces.

Sensor: SW-420 Vibration Sensor​

The SW-420 Vibration Sensor is a small and sensitive module designed to detect vibrations and movement. It typically consists of a spring-mounted metal ball inside a metal casing, creating an electrical connection. When subjected to vibrations or shocks, the ball may move and momentarily disrupt this connection, generating an electrical signal. This signal can be detected and processed by a microcontroller or other electronic circuits to detect and respond to vibrations or impacts. The SW-420 Vibration Sensor is commonly used in applications like burglar alarms, vehicle security systems, and industrial equipment to trigger actions in response to physical movement or tampering. Its simplicity and effectiveness make it a valuable component for projects that require motion sensing and security features.

Technical Specifications and Features:​

  • Operating Voltage: Typically operates within a voltage range between 3.3V and 5V.
  • Sensitivity: The sensor's sensitivity to vibrations and shocks can vary, specified in terms of the minimum force required to trigger a response.
  • Output: Generates a digital signal (typically HIGH or LOW) when vibrations are detected.
  • Contact Type: Utilizes a spring-mounted metal ball inside a metal casing to detect vibrations by momentarily disrupting an electrical connection.
  • Mounting: Can be easily mounted to surfaces where vibration detection is required.
  • Dimensions: Comes in various sizes and form factors, with commonly used dimensions for easy integration into projects.

References:​

  • SW-420 Vibration Sensor Datasheet

Actuator: LED​

LEDs, or Light Emitting Diodes, are semiconductor devices that emit light when an electric current is applied. Known for their energy efficiency, compact size, and long life, LEDs are extensively used across various applications including electronic devices, display panels, indicators, and general lighting. Their ability to be manufactured in various colors and sizes makes LEDs exceptionally versatile for design and functional purposes in numerous projects.

Technical Specifications and Features​

  • Dice Material: Gallium Arsenide Phosphide on Gallium Phosphide (GaAsP on GaP/GaP).
  • Emitted Colors: Available in Green and Red.
  • Lens Color: Green Diffused and Red Diffused.
  • Peak Wavelength:
    • Green: 568 nm
    • Red: 587 nm
  • Viewing Angle: 45 degrees, offering focused illumination.
  • Luminous Intensity (IV):
    • Green: 15 mcd
    • Red: 14 mcd

References​

  • Red LED Datasheet

Actuator: Buzzer​

A passive piezoelectric buzzer utilizes a piezoelectric crystal or ceramic element to create sound. When alternating voltage is applied, the piezoelectric element undergoes rapid expansion and contraction, which generates vibrations. These vibrations produce sound waves, creating audible noise. This type of buzzer is commonly used in alarms, timers, and user interface feedback due to its simplicity and effectiveness.

Technical Specifications and Features​

  • Input Voltage (Max.): 5V
  • Resistance: 42 Ω
  • Resonance Frequency: 2048 Hz
  • Body Size: 12mm x 8mm
  • Pin Pitch: 6mm
  • External Material: Plastic
  • Color: Black

Communication Protocols​

Controller Communication​

The communication between the Raspberry Pi Pico W controller and the ThingsBoard server involves two key protocols: Wi-Fi and MQTT.

Wi-Fi Connection:​

The Raspberry Pi Pico W connects to a local Wi-Fi network, enabling it to access the internet. This connection provides the foundation for communication with external services like the ThingsBoard server.

MQTT Protocol:​

The Message Queuing Telemetry Transport (MQTT) protocol is employed for transmitting data between the controller and the ThingsBoard server. MQTT is a lightweight and efficient protocol designed for remote devices with limited resources. It facilitates the publication of data (such as access events) from the controller to the server, as well as the subscription to commands or acknowledgements from the server. MQTT ensures secure and reliable communication, making it ideal for real-time data visualization and monitoring on the ThingsBoard dashboard. The controller publishes messages related to access events, which are then visualized on the dashboard, offering insights into the system's performance and access patterns.

ensor Communication​

The communication between the SW-420 vibration sensor and the Raspberry Pi Pico W controller is accomplished through digital data.

Digital Data Communication:​

The SW-420 vibration sensor is designed to provide digital output. It communicates with the controller by generating a digital signal based on the presence or absence of vibrations or shocks. When a vibration or shock is detected, the sensor produces a digital signal that the controller can readily interpret. This digital signal serves as an input to the controller's GPIO pins, allowing it to react promptly to detected events. This digital communication method ensures the swift and accurate detection of vibrations and shocks, making it suitable for the node's purpose.
